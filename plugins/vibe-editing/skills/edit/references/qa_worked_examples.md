# Q&A WORKED EXAMPLES — the gold standard to pattern-match (study BEFORE cutting)

> These are APPROVED cuts (Operator: "very, very good") generated from Tier1 raw with the SOP — and they land
> within a hair of how the reference editor actually cut the same footage. **When cutting a new Q&A, pattern-match to
> these, not to abstract rules.** Each shows: the cut, why each beat earns its place, the fundamentals
> score, and the cold-start mistake it avoids. (The bar is 85%+ on fundamentals + "is it a good clip" —
> NOT transcript-match to the reference editor, who is inconsistent and whose guests are all different.)
>
> Add a new example here every time a cut clears the bar in a review — the set is the calibration memory.

## EXAMPLE 1 — Guest, golf carts (~68s) — score ~89/100
**Raw shape:** guest sells golf carts, 3 locations, newest is "bleeding," thinks the constraint is lead-gen;
Speaker prescribes a giveaway campaign; then a long swamp monologue + an "Ed" outro follow (different segment).

**THE CUT:**
> "I'm Guest, I sell golf carts. Probably the lamest thing in here, but it's a lot of fun. I have three
> locations now, one launched in the last year — and it is bleeding. I'm putting money into it every month
> that I haven't seen back. My biggest constraint is lead generation. *[Speaker]* Any reason you don't just run
> local ads? *[Guest]* I need to. *[Speaker]* Local's the easiest way to get customers, and it's a high-ticket
> product, so you're in this weird exclusionary area where it can totally work. I bet your LTV-to-CAC is
> exceptional because your content's fun. So here's what I'd do: run a giveaway campaign. 'We're giving away
> this souped-up insane golf cart to one family in the hyper-local area — register here, drawing in 30 days.'
> You run that ad, collect all the leads, second-place winner gets $2,000 off. Every person who opts in,
> asterisk: more than one winner can be selected. And because it's a giveaway, you can ask for a ton of
> friction on the way in — income, address, everything you'd want to know. **And you'll probably sell more
> than a hundred carts. That's what I'd do.**"

**Why it works, beat by beat:**
- HOOK = mundane-contrast ("golf carts… lamest thing in here, but it's a lot of fun") + immediate stakes
  ("it is bleeding"). Self-deprecation is the contrast lift — KEEP it.
- ONE problem (lead-gen) → ONE solution (giveaway). No second thread.
- Reasoning ladder kept: *why* local, *why* high-ticket works, *why* a giveaway (LTV/fun content) — that's
  what makes the tactic land instead of feeling random.
- **ENDS on the quantified result: "more than a hundred carts."** Not the label.
- **Cold-start mistake avoided:** the cold session ran this ~128s (kept the swamp monologue) and ended on
  the restatement "it's a lead-gen campaign." Both are killers — cut the detour, end on the number.

## EXAMPLE 2 — Nedu, overnight baby care (~95s) — score ~93/100
**Raw shape:** franchisee, $1.2M across 4 territories (~$300K profit), keeps a full-time day job out of fear;
Speaker reframes the *risk* (hiring eats the profit vs. just going pro) and closes on the commitment.

**THE CUT:**
> "We provide overnight care for babies. Our revenue last year was 1.2 — it's a franchise, so I have four
> territories, each does about $300,000. On top of that, I have a full-time daytime job. *[Speaker]* Does that
> pay better than the franchise? *[Nedu]* No. *[Speaker]* Why are you doing that? *[Nedu]* I don't know.
> *[Speaker]* You must have a lot of work ethic and skill — you built four territories while keeping a full-time
> job. So take that in for a second. The alternative is you're not making enough profit. You've got $300,000,
> which is a lot. But if you tried to hire somebody to do that eight-hour-a-day job, you'd eat up the majority
> of that profit finding that good person. So to me, that's actually *riskier* than you leaving the job and
> doing it yourself — spending eight more hours when you already know how. Your day becomes talking on the
> phone to new moms and nannies. That's the job. If you did that eight more hours a day, could you replace
> your income? *[Nedu]* Oh, yeah. **[Speaker] Then let's do that.**"

**Why it works, beat by beat:**
- HOOK = activity→revenue ("overnight care for babies… 1.2… four territories"). Clean contrast.
- The TENSION is the day-job fear; the "why are you doing that? / I don't know" exchange is gold — keep it.
- Reasoning ladder is the whole clip: **the risk-reframe** ($300K eaten by a hire vs. DIY) is the WHY that
  earns the close. Do NOT amputate this to bookends.
- **ENDS on the decision/commitment: "Then let's do that."** A resolved principle (the safer bet is you),
  not an open question.
- **Cold-start mistake avoided:** the cold session stopped one beat early on the open question "which risk
  sounds bigger?" — leaving it unresolved. Go the one beat forward to the commitment.

## THE PATTERN (what both examples prove)
1. Open on the contrast hook (what they do → the number / the mundane-but-real stakes). Drop "Sweet"/greetings.
2. Keep Speaker's *reasoning ladder* — the diagnosis + the WHY. That's the substance, not filler.
3. Cut every diagnostic detour the moment the answer is reached. No second thread, no swamp tangent, no outro.
4. **End HARD on the portable principle / quantified result / commitment** — never a tactic, wind-down, open
   question, or restatement of the problem.
5. ~70–120s for most; let dense ones run to ~150. Never a sub-40s skeleton, never padded.
